Introduction

    This module has equipped me with a practical understanding of research methodologies, essential for conducting effective research in real-world settings. Through hands-on experience with various research tools and techniques, coupled with valuable feedback on case studies and project tasks, I've developed a strong foundation in the research process. Furthermore, the module emphasised the critical legal, social, ethical, and professional considerations within computing projects, including relevant codes of practice. By engaging with core textbooks grounded in the scientific method and tailored for diverse organisational contexts, I've honed my ability to identify research practicalities and critically analyse fundamental research principles, connecting this knowledge to insights gained in other programme modules. The continuous assessment, featuring case studies and milestone evaluations, fostered both peer and instructor interaction. This e-Portfolio serves as a testament to my growth throughout this module, showcasing my abilities and insights through collected evidence and personal reflections.

Unit 1: Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI

    This unit involved reflecting on the burgeoning field of AI and the critical need for ethical guidelines. I analysed the historical progression of AI, noting the cycles of progress and setbacks (Spector, 2006), and the impact of Big Data on its recent acceleration (Duan, Edwards & Dwivedi, 2019). The diverse approaches to AI regulation across countries, from the EU's risk-based framework (European Commission, 2019) to Asia's innovation-control balance and Africa's emerging discourse (Kiemdi et al., 2022), were examined. I learned about the complexities of establishing global ethical standards and the importance of inclusivity and long-term impact considerations (Deckard, 2023). This unit enhanced my critical thinking skills regarding the societal implications of technology.

Unit 2: Collaborative Discussion on Ethical Dilemmas

    This unit involved participating in a collaborative discussion analysing various ethical dilemmas in technology and the workplace. We examined scenarios involving workplace harassment, where a leader's abusive behaviour was dismissed, highlighting the need for ethical training and reporting systems. Unethical business practices, such as a web hosting company enabling cybercrime for profit, raised questions about corporate responsibility. The deceptive use of dark UX patterns underscored the importance of ethical design principles. Finally, a data security breach at a medical startup emphasised the necessity of continuous security audits. Through this unit, I improved my ability to identify and analyse complex ethical issues in professional contexts and understand the importance of adhering to codes of conduct. - See collaborative discussion on unit 2 as evidence.

Unit 3: Peer Reviews of AI and Big Data Studies

    In this unit, I analysed two peer reviews of distinct research studies. The first review critiqued a survey on AI usage in radiology (Allen et al., 2021), questioning the appropriateness of the MOORA method and the clarity of ranking criteria. The second review assessed a study on epileptic seizure prediction using big data and deep learning (Kiral-Kornek et al., 2018), acknowledging its methodology but noting limitations in the dataset's size and representativeness. I learned to critically evaluate research methodologies, identify potential weaknesses in data analysis and interpretation, and understand the importance of representative datasets in scientific studies. This unit honed my analytical and critical appraisal skills.

Unit 4: Case Study: Ethical Considerations in Data Access

    Unit 4 analysed a case study about a researcher's request for property tax records. I explored a records clerk's (Ricardo) ethical duties in granting access, considering privacy and confidentiality (GDPR, 2018; ICO, 2020), including informed consent, data minimisation, and public trust. I learned the legal/ethical frameworks for data handling and balancing research needs with individual rights. Further analysis covered ethical issues of a researcher (Beth) contacting individuals. I learned about informed consent (GDPR, 2018) and purpose limitation (ICO, 2020). Releasing data without consent risks identity theft (BMA, 2021) and erodes trust. Mixed responses require respecting denials. If ethical access is impossible, study scope should be reconsidered. Core principles are privacy, consent, transparency, minimisation, and public trust (GDPR, 2018; ICO, 2020; BMA, 2021), outweighing research convenience. This unit deepened my understanding of ethical data handling.

Unit 5: Case Study: Misuse of Surveys

    This unit centred on the ethical implications of survey misuse, using the Cambridge Analytica scandal as a prime example (Confessore, 2018; Hinds et al., 2020). I examined the motivations behind the data harvesting and its use for political manipulation, highlighting the violation of trust and consent. The social, legal, and professional repercussions of this scandal, including the erosion of trust in social media and the impetus for stricter data privacy laws, were analysed. Additionally, the unit explored the misuse of the Korean National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (KNHANES) due to improper statistical methods (Kim et al., 2013), leading to potentially harmful misinformation. I learned about the serious consequences of unethical data collection and analysis and the importance of rigorous statistical practices in survey research.

Unit 6: Inferential Statistics Workshop Interpretations

    This unit involved interpreting results from statistical workshops focusing on inferential statistics. I analysed the comparison of two diets, determining that Diet A led to greater and more consistent weight loss based on mean, standard deviation, and interquartile range (Worksheets 7.1B & 7.2B). Frequency analysis revealed the dominance of the "Other" category in a dataset (Worksheet 7.3D). A t-test comparing two conditions showed a statistically significant difference, with one performing better, while also noting variability across stores (Worksheet 7.4F). Through this unit, I gained practical experience in interpreting descriptive statistics, understanding the outcomes of hypothesis testing (t-tests), and identifying meaningful patterns and differences within datasets. This enhanced my data interpretation skills.

Unit 7: Hypothesis Testing Using Excel

    This unit focused on applying hypothesis testing using Excel. I interpreted the results of a one-tailed paired t-test that provided strong evidence for the greater effectiveness of Filter Agent 1 over Agent 2 (Exercise 7.1). I also analysed a paired two-sample t-test on bank cardholder income data, which indicated a statistically significant difference in mean income between males and females (Exercise 7.2), while noting the underlying assumptions of the test. Furthermore, a two-tailed paired t-test on filtration data revealed a significant difference in mean impurity levels between two agents, with Agent 2 being more effective (Exercise 7.3). This unit solidified my understanding of different types of t-tests and their application in drawing conclusions from data using statistical software.

Unit 8: Data Analysis and Visualisation

    This unit involved revisiting previous datasets to perform further data analysis and visualization. Analysing the weight loss dataset again, both descriptive statistics and a t-test confirmed the significant advantage of Diet A, which was visually represented in a bar chart. The analysis of brand preferences in two demographic areas showed a preference for the "Other" brand in both, with a Chi-Square Test of Independence indicating no significant association between brand preference and demographic area. Reanalysing the filtration dataset suggested that while mean impurity levels were close, Agent 1 was statistically more effective. This unit reinforced my skills in applying various statistical techniques and using visualisation tools to communicate findings effectively.

Unit 9: Workbook Interpretations

    This unit focused on interpreting and visualising data presented in workbooks. Bar charts effectively illustrated brand preferences in two areas, highlighting the popularity of the "Other" brand in both. Analysis of heather species distribution revealed significant differences between two locations, with Location A showing more abundant growth. Comparing weight loss patterns using relative frequency histograms visually demonstrated the greater variability in outcomes for Diet B. Finally, analysing brand preferences across demographic areas showed Brand A's significant dominance in Area 2. This unit further developed my ability to interpret visual representations of data and draw meaningful conclusions from different types of charts and statistical analyses.

Unit 10: Research proposal presentation

    In this unit, I delivered a research proposal presentation on systematically comparing Vision Transformers and GANs for breast cancer subtype classification using histopathology images from the TCGA-BRCA dataset. I aimed to address limitations in current research, focusing on reproducibility and comprehensive evaluation beyond just accuracy. My objectives included a direct comparison, developing a robust evaluation framework, quantifying the impact of GAN augmentation on ViTs, ensuring full reproducibility through open-source code and containerisation, and providing evidence-based recommendations. I felt I needed to perform exceptionally well in this assignment to compensate for my lower literature review grade, so I meticulously incorporated all feedback from my tutor into this presentation.
